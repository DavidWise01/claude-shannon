CLAUDE SHANNON (1916–2001) — LOGIKĒ XII, THE CLOSER, where 2,500 years of logic lands on the switch. Boole made logic an algebra of 0/1; eighty years on a 21-year-old MIT student wrote what's been called the most important master's thesis ever, and it was one devastating observation: ⚑ A SWITCH IS A BIT. An electrical switch is open(0)/closed(1); two in SERIES conduct iff both closed = AND; two in PARALLEL conduct iff either closed = OR; a broken switch = NOT — so Boole's algebra of thought IS LITERALLY the algebra of circuits ('A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits', 1937). That means any logical function can be BUILT from switches and, crucially, MINIMISED by algebra into fewer parts = the birth of the LOGIC GATE & all digital design. A decade later he did it again: ⚑ INFORMATION THEORY ('A Mathematical Theory of Communication', 1948) — he gave information a precise measure (ENTROPY H=−Σp·log₂p) and named the BIT, fixing the hard floor on compression & the ceiling on channel capacity. LIVE: (1) a SWITCHING CIRCUIT — toggle switches A/B, pick series(AND)/parallel(OR)/invert(NOT), the SVG lamp lights iff current flows + the Boolean expression & truth table update (verified); (2) an EXACT BOOLEAN MINIMISER — click a truth table's outputs to define any F(A,B), watch naïve sum-of-products vs the algebra-minimal form, gate count falling (A·B+A·B̄=A; OR 3→2 terms — and HONEST when it can't, e.g. XOR stays 2; exact-cover verified); (3) an ENTROPY METER — drag a coin's bias p → H(p) bits, max 1 at p=½, 0 at certainty (verified H(0.5)=1, H(0.25)=0.811). CONFIDENCE-TIERED: STRONG (switching algebra & information theory — rigorous, foundational, between them they define how every computer computes & every channel communicates) / BUILT-ON-BOOLE (the 1937 algebra is BOOLE's — Shannon's genius was the BRIDGE, seeing it IS switching; the 1948 theory he built near-from-scratch) / 'BIT'-HAS-LIMITS (⚠ Shannon information = uncertainty/message-length, NOT meaning/truth/worth — a page of noise has high entropy & no sense; reading the everyday word into the equation is the classic error).
